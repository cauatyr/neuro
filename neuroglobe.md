**Slide 1:**
5 MENTAL HACKS THAT WILL SKYROCKET YOUR MEMORY
SWIPE

**Slide 2:**
MEMORY PALACE

Imagine a familiar place, like your home. Associate each room or object with information you want to remember. To recall, mentally walk through your "palace," retrieving information as you go.

This technique uses spatial memory, activating the hippocampus (responsible for memory) by linking new information with familiar locations. This makes retrieval easier by creating multiple associations.

**Slide 3:**
THE FEYNMAN TECHNIQUE

Pretend to teach a concept as if explaining it to a child. Break it down into simple terms. When you hit a gap in your understanding, revisit the material until you can explain it smoothly.

This method enhances memory encoding by actively engaging with the material and reinforcing neural pathways. Teaching a concept uses deep processing, increasing retention through more detailed understanding.

**Slide 4:**
MIND MAPPING

Write down a central concept and connect related ideas in branches. Visually organize information to see relationships and hierarchy.

Mind mapping utilizes both the left (analytical) and right (creative) hemispheres, enhancing memory through dual coding (visual and verbal). The structured approach helps in organizing information, facilitating easier retrieval.

**Slide 5:**
LINK METHOD

Take a series of items or ideas and create a story that links them together in a vivid, imaginative sequence. For example, if you need to remember "apple, book, and shoe," imagine an apple balancing on a book inside a giant shoe.

This method leverages associative memory, strengthening neural networks by creating mental "chains" between concepts. The process of linking and visualizing engages multiple brain areas, such as the visual cortex and prefrontal cortex, improving recall by connecting ideas with visual cues.

**Slide 6:**
SLEEP ENHANCED LEARNING

Study right before going to sleep or take a nap after learning something new. Sleep on it without additional study or stimulation.

During sleep, particularly deep and REM stages, the brain consolidates memory by replaying and reinforcing neural connections. The hippocampus and neocortex work together to stabilize and integrate new information, solidifying learning and strengthening recall.

**Slide 2:**

Spend a full day with minimal distractions. No screens, no processed snacks, no artificial stimulants (even caffeine).

A single dopamine fast is like hitting the reset button for your brain, helping you recognize and value the little things again.

**Slide 4:**

Eat foods rich in tyrosine, an amino acid that helps your brain produce dopamine naturally.

Examples: Eggs, almonds, avocados, salmon, and dark chocolate.

**Slide 5:**

Physical activity floods your brain with healthy dopamine and endorphins. Even 20 minutes of walking or stretching can help.

For maximum dopamine production, try HIIT workouts or cardio, which give your brain the strongest boost.

Regular movement rewires your brain to produce dopamine in a healthy, consistent way.
